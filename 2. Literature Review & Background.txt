2. Literature Review & Background
2.1 The Evolution of AI Music Generation
The history of AI-generated music stretches back decades, evolving through several distinct technological paradigms — from rule-based algorithmic systems to modern deep learning architectures capable of generating rich, full-quality audio. Understanding this progression is essential to appreciating where SonicAI stands in the broader research landscape and what gaps it is designed to address.
The earliest computational approaches to music generation relied on algorithmic composition — explicitly programmed rules derived from music theory to produce structured MIDI sequences. While these methods offered a degree of creative output, they were rigid, limited in expressive range, and dependent entirely on the quality of hand-crafted rules. The introduction of deep learning fundamentally changed this trajectory.
Transformer-based models have been widely applied in polyphonic music generation. Huang et al. combined the relative attention mechanism into the Transformer and proposed the Music Transformer, which can generate long-term music structures of up to 2,000 tokens, generate continuations that coherently elaborate on a given motif, and generate accompaniments given a melody — marking the first successful application of the Transformer architecture to long-term structured music generation. Building directly on this foundation, OpenAI introduced MuseNet in 2019, a landmark model in symbolic music generation. MuseNet was not explicitly programmed with human understanding of music, but instead discovered patterns of harmony, rhythm, and style by learning to predict the next token in hundreds of thousands of MIDI files. MusicGen MuseNet is a deep neural network that can generate four-minute musical compositions with ten different instruments, combining styles ranging from country to Mozart to the Beatles. Meta Despite its creative breadth, MuseNet operated in the symbolic MIDI domain — producing structured note representations rather than actual audio waveforms. The resulting outputs required additional rendering pipelines before they could be heard as real music, and the model offered users limited control or understanding over the generative process.
OpenAI's subsequent work, Jukebox (2020), pushed into raw audio territory, marking a significant leap. Jukebox is a neural network that generates music, including rudimentary singing, as raw audio in a variety of genres and artist styles using a quantization-based approach called VQ-VAE to compress audio into a discrete latent space. Medium While Jukebox demonstrated that high-fidelity audio generation was achievable, its computational requirements were prohibitive and the model lacked interactive, user-controllable generation features. Users remained passive observers of outputs they could not meaningfully shape or learn from.

2.2 Meta AI's MusicGen — The State of the Art
The most significant recent advancement in controllable music generation is Meta AI's MusicGen (Copet et al., 2023), which forms the core AI engine of SonicAI. MusicGen represents a fundamental architectural shift from earlier multi-stage pipelines. Previous text-to-music systems required cascading several specialized models — one for melody, one for rhythm, one for timbre — each introducing compounding errors. MusicGen eliminates this complexity entirely.
MusicGen employs a single-stage autoregressive Transformer that operates directly over discrete audio tokens produced by Meta's EnCodec — a neural audio codec that compresses audio at 32kHz into compact discrete representations. This design allows the model to generate coherent, high-fidelity audio in a single forward pass without the brittleness of multi-model pipelines. The model is available in three sizes — 300M, 1.5B, and 3.3B parameters — providing a practical range from lightweight inference on consumer hardware to high-quality generation on GPU-equipped servers. MusicGen was trained on approximately 20,000 hours of licensed music, drawing from proprietary Meta datasets as well as Shutterstock and Pond5 music collections, ensuring both diversity and legal compliance in training data.
What distinguishes MusicGen for SonicAI's purposes is its support for text-conditioned generation — the ability to accept natural language descriptions and produce musically coherent outputs that reflect genre, mood, tempo, and instrumentation as described. This makes it the ideal engine for a platform where users express creative intent through plain language rather than technical musical commands.

2.3 Bark — Vocal and Audio Synthesis
To complement MusicGen's instrumental generation capabilities, SonicAI's hybrid pipeline optionally incorporates Bark, Suno AI's open-source text-to-audio model for vocal synthesis. Bark is a fully generative text-to-audio model following a GPT-style architecture similar to AudioLM and Vall-E, using a quantized audio representation from EnCodec. Unlike conventional text-to-speech models, Bark converts input text directly to audio without intermediate phoneme representations. ResearchGate
Bark consists of three sequential transformer models. In the first stage, input text is tokenized using the BERT tokenizer and mapped to semantic tokens that capture the intended acoustic content, including both verbal and nonverbal elements. These semantic tokens are then transformed into coarse audio tokens corresponding to the primary codebooks of the EnCodec representation. In the final stage, the model refines these into fine EnCodec tokens, enabling high-fidelity reconstruction of complex audio waveforms. 2.4 Limitations of Existing Tools and the Research Gap
Despite the impressive technical capabilities of MusicGen, Bark, Jukebox, and commercial platforms such as Suno Studio and Udio, a critical gap persists across all of them — they function as black-box generation tools. The user provides input and receives output, with no transparency into the musical decisions made during generation. The evaluation of symbolic musical generation models is mostly based on low-level mathematical metrics due to the inherent difficulty in measuring musical quality, given the subjective nature of music. Honu More importantly for educational purposes, no existing tool uses the generation process as an opportunity to transfer musical knowledge to the user.
This gap is significant. A user who generates a Lo-Fi track using Suno finishes the interaction knowing no more about pentatonic scales, syncopated rhythms, or Rhodes piano timbres than when they started. The technology accelerates output but contributes nothing to the user's musical literacy or creative development. For students, hobbyists, aspiring producers, and educators, this represents a missed opportunity of enormous scale.
SonicAI addresses this gap directly. By integrating a structured pedagogical workflow — where AI-generated learning plans, interactive customization of musical parameters, and final audio generation are combined into a single experience — SonicAI transforms the act of music generation into an act of music education. It builds upon the technical foundations established by MusicGen and Bark while introducing an entirely new application layer: one that treats every generated track as a teaching moment, and every user as a learner in the process of becoming a creator.
